Quick Reference — Key Statistics & Quotes
Family Costs — Key Statistics
  • $40B+ — U.S. family spending on youth sports annually; twice the cash flow of the NFL (Aspen Institute, Feb 2025)
  • +46% — Cost increase since 2019; average family spends $1,016 on a child's primary sport — double U.S. inflation (Aspen Institute, Feb 2025)
  • +105% — Basketball cost increase in 5 years; soccer +69%, baseball +68% (Aspen Institute, Feb 2025)
  • $25,000 — High end of annual family spending reported in the 2024 Aspen survey
  • 75% of parents considered pulling kids from sports; 1 in 5 took on debt (Good Sports/Harris Poll)
  • 25% of travel sports parents pulled from savings or emergency fund (New York Life, 2025)
  • 20.2 pp — Income participation gap: wealthy kids nearly 2× as likely to play organized sports (Project Play 2025)
  • $154.5B — Projected global youth sports market by 2035, up from $56B in 2025 (Business Research Insights)
Social Media — Key Statistics
  • 80%+ of athletes use social media 2+ hours/day; mean of 4 hours daily (Sports Psychiatry/Hogrefe, Nov 2023)
  • 72% of fans say TikTok motivates them to physically play sports (Stagwell/NRG, Jan 2026)
  • 62% prefer watching sports on social media over traditional TV (Stagwell/NRG, Jan 2026)
  • 31% of those surveyed cite social media pressure and public recognition as a measure of success (BSN Sports / Talker Research, 2025)
NIL Deals — Key Statistics
  • $2.75B — NIL market in 2025–26, nearly triple its $917M launch in 2021 (Opendorse "NIL at 4," Jul 2025; a separate DraftWorth estimate puts 2026 at $2.6B)
  • $10–12M — Reported 4-year deal for 17-year-old QB Bryce Underwood to flip from LSU to Michigan — the richest recruiting flip in college football history (Fortune / Front Office Sports, 2025)
  • Age 13 — McKenna "Mak" Whitham, the youngest athlete in any sport signed by Nike to an NIL deal (ESPN, 2024); a 9-year-old in Snoop Dogg's youth league reportedly signed a six-figure deal (KTLA, Jul 2023)
  • 45 states + D.C. now allow some form of NIL for high school athletes (2aDays, Dec 2025)

Family Costs in
Youth Sports

Average family spending on a child's primary sport rose 46% from 2019 to 2024, about double U.S. inflation over the same period. Total U.S. family spending is an estimated $40 billion a year; individual outlays range from near zero to about $25,000.

High School NIL Adoption: State-by-State Rollout (2021–2025)
Number of U.S. jurisdictions (states + DC) permitting some form of high school athlete NIL deals
Sources: 2aDays (December 2025 — confirmed 45 states + DC = 46 jurisdictions); NFHS state-level tracking; NIL platform legal databases. The NCAA's 2021 ruling covered college athletes only — individual states had to pass separate HS legislation. Florida and Louisiana were early movers; adoption accelerated through 2023–24. With 45 states permitting some form of HS NIL, roughly 5 states still hold out as of late 2025.
