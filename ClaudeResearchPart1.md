SECTION 1: SOCIAL MEDIA & ONLINE CULTURE IN YOUTH SPORTS

Teens specifically go to social media to follow athletes — it's a core driver of platform use

"majorities of teens on Instagram and TikTok say they go there to keep up with athletes or celebrities. This falls to 37% among those on Snapchat."

— Pew Research Center, Teens' Experiences on TikTok, Instagram and Snapchat (April 2026). https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2026/04/15/teens-experiences-on-tiktok-instagram-and-snapchat/


Sports content consumption has shifted decisively from TV to social — and TikTok actively drives kids to play

"62% of sports fans enjoy watching sports content on social media more than on traditional TV. 72% say TikTok, in particular, motivates them to play... 50% prefer watching short clips, highlights or behind-the-scenes moments over full games (60% for Gen Z)."

— Stagwell / National Research Group (Jan 2026). https://www.stagwellglobal.com/what-the-data-say-62-enjoy-watching-sports-on-social-media-more-than-on-tv/


Athletes consume far more social media than the average person

"over 80% of athletes reported two or more hours of social media use daily, with a mean of 4 hours of use per day."

— Sports Psychiatry (Hogrefe), Nov 2023. https://econtent.hogrefe.com/doi/10.1024/2674-0052/a000060 


Social media is now a formal, expected part of the recruiting pipeline — a structural change in the culture

"If you are a serious recruit in 2026, you are not just being evaluated on the field, you are being evaluated on your phone. College coaches are on X, Instagram, TikTok, and YouTube every day, and they are absolutely checking your profiles."

— Pathley, Social Media for Recruiting (Feb 2026). https://www.pathley.ai/blog-posts/social-media-for-recruiting-guide 





Coaches openly say social media is now how they discover talent

"More than ever, social media is important," says Gettysburg head basketball coach B.J. Dunne. "When we're not physically able to go to events, it's another way for us to dig up some names and do some research."

— NCSA College Recruiting (Oct 2025). https://www.ncsasports.org/recruiting/contacting-college-coaches/social-media 


The "followers = money" culture (this is the strongest cultural-shift evidence)
Follower counts have literally become a unit of currency for young athletes

"a tweet would generate athletes $10 from every 1,000 followers, $3-$4 per follower via TikTok, and $4-$7 per follower through YouTube" — per Opendorse CEO Blake Lawrence

— Newsweek / AP (June 2021). https://www.newsweek.com/college-athletes-could-make-20-per-instagram-follower-some-nil-laws-take-effect-1605611 


A single college athlete turned 8 million TikTok followers into millions — the model kids now chase

"It's the path former LSU gymnast Livvy Dunne pioneered as she racked up 8 million TikTok followers and millions in endorsement deals before announcing her retirement from her sport last spring."

— Like and Subscribe / Substack (Oct 2025). https://likeandsubscribenews.substack.com/p/the-nil-gold-rush-how-499000-college 


Roughly half a million college athletes are now effectively content creators

"The NIL Gold Rush: How 499,000 College Athletes are Becoming Creators"

— Like and Subscribe / Substack (Oct 2025). https://likeandsubscribenews.substack.com/p/the-nil-gold-rush-how-499000-college  Substack 

The TikTok-to-deal pipeline is so real a high schooler/college athlete built ~$1M in deals largely off it

"Sam Hurley has built a massive following with his charisma, chiseled abs, and athletic prowess and parlayed it in NIL deals" — earning in the neighborhood of $1 million.

— TIME (June 2023). https://time.com/6284880/sam-hurley-track-and-field-tiktok-nil-texas/ 


The documented downside / cultural-cost angle (great for a "this changed childhood" argument)
Kids are now recorded from the moment they start organized sports

"Child athletes are being recorded now from the moment they join organized sports."

— Aspen Institute Project Play, State of Play 2025 (10 Trends to Watch). https://projectplay.org/state-of-play-2025/10-youth-sports-trends-to-watch 
 

Nearly a third of youth sports participants now measure their own success by social media presence

"Another 31% measure success by social media presence and public" [recognition]

— BSN Sports Survey via Youth Sports Business Report (Nov 2025). https://youthsportsbusinessreport.com/one-in-six-parents-expect-their-child-to-become-a-pro-athlete-new-bsn-survey-reveals/ 


Curated highlight culture is creating a comparison/anxiety problem among young athletes

"A whopping 46 percent of teens reported social media made them feel worse about themselves. 'Comparison can be a really big problem, especially for athletes'"

— USA Lacrosse / TrueSport (Oct 2024). https://www.usalacrosse.com/news-media-blog/parents-pros-and-cons-social-media-use-young-athletes 


The "highlight machine" framing — a sharp, quotable cultural critique

"Social media functions as a highlight machine that selects extreme positive outcomes and filters out everything else."

— Dr. Paul McCarthy (April 2026). https://www.drpaulmccarthy.com/post/how-to-protect-young-athletes-from-social-media-pressure-a-step-by-step-guide-for-parents-and-coach 
SECTION 2: THE COST OF YOUTH SPORTS
 


The headline numbers
 
The single most-cited stat — average spend and the 46% surge (this is THE number)

"The average U.S. sports family spent $1,016 on their child's primary sport in 2024, a 46% increase since 2019, according to the Aspen Institute's latest parent survey in partnership with Utah State University and Louisiana Tech University."

— Project Play / Aspen Institute (Feb 2025). https://projectplay.org/news/2025/2/24/project-play-survey-family-spending-on-youth-sports-rises-46-over-five-years 


The cost surge is DOUBLE inflation — a great "out of control" framing

"That's twice the rate of price inflations in the U.S. economy during the same period."

— Project Play / Aspen Institute, State of Play 2025 (10 Trends). https://projectplay.org/state-of-play-2025/10-youth-sports-trends-to-watch 


The true per-child total is closer to $1,500 once you count their other sports

"The average U.S. sports family spent $1,016 on their child's primary sport in 2024, and nearly $1,500 for all of that child's sports experiences, according to the Aspen Institute."

— Charles Schwab (April 2025). https://www.schwab.com/learn/story/are-kids-sports-costs-straining-your-budget 


The total economy is now ~$40B+ — bigger than the NFL

"The $40 billion now spent by families on youth sports is twice the amount of cash flowing through the NFL, the most lucrative sports league in the world, and does not include the public spend by schools and municipalities, or that of private facility operators and sponsors."

— Project Play / Aspen Institute (Feb 2025). https://projectplay.org/news/2025/2/24/project-play-survey-family-spending-on-youth-sports-rises-46-over-five-years 

This directly supports your boss's "Aspen average undersells it" point — the range goes up to ~$25,000

"In the 2024 survey, parents estimated their sports costs ranged from nothing to almost $25,000."

— Project Play / Aspen Institute (Feb 2025). https://projectplay.org/news/2025/2/24/project-play-survey-family-spending-on-youth-sports-rises-46-over-five-years 


Cost growth by specific sport (shocking percentage jumps)
Basketball costs MORE THAN DOUBLED in five years

"Within five years, the average basketball parent spent 105% more on their child in that sport. Parents in soccer (69%) and baseball (68%) also paid significantly more."

— Project Play / Aspen Institute (Feb 2025). https://projectplay.org/news/2025/2/24/project-play-survey-family-spending-on-youth-sports-rises-46-over-five-years 



The raw dollar jumps by sport (2019 → 2024)

"Since 2019, average annual family spending on baseball climbed from $660 to $1,113, while soccer rose from $537 to $910 and basketball increased from $427 to $876."

— Kiplinger (citing Project Play, ~May 2026). https://www.kiplinger.com/personal-finance/family-savings/what-families-are-really-spending-to-keep-kids-in-sports 


Spending jumps sharply with age — nearly $2,000/year for teens

"families spending nearly $2,000 annually on teens ages 15 to 18."

— Kiplinger (citing Project Play). https://www.kiplinger.com/personal-finance/family-savings/what-families-are-really-spending-to-keep-kids-in-sports 


Travel and club sports (where the $15K figure lives — your boss's number)
The exact "$15,000" travel baseball figure your boss referenced, attributed

"At the high end of competitive travel programs, total annual spend reaches $10,000 to $15,000 per player when everything is counted."

— Baseball Mode (citing Beyond the Dugout 2025 + Project Play, Oct 2024). https://baseballmode.com/blogs/blog/travel-baseball-101 


Another travel baseball source confirms the up-to-$15K range

"Travel baseball costs vary, but families should expect to spend between $4,000 – $15,000 per season."

— North Texas Select Baseball (March 2025). https://www.ntxselectbaseball.com/the-true-cost-of-travel-baseball-fees-travel-and-hidden-expenses/ 


Travel is now the single costliest line item — even averaged across ALL kids

"Travel is now the costliest feature in youth sports. On average across all sports, parents spent more annually on travel ($260 per sport, per child) than equipment ($154), private lessons ($183), registration fees ($168), and camps ($111)."

— Project Play / Aspen Institute, Youth Sports Facts: Challenges. https://projectplay.org/youth-sports/facts/challenges 


One marquee tournament alone (Cooperstown) costs ~$1,900 just for entry

"Cooperstown Dreams Park (a bucket-list tournament for 12U players) costs approximately $1,900 per player just for entry, plus all your family's travel expenses to New York. Perfect Game showcases for older players seeking college exposure run $700-$1,000 per event."

— eSportsInsurance (April 2025). https://esportsinsurance.com/how-much-does-it-cost-to-play-travel-baseball/ 


Hockey is the most expensive sport overall

"Hockey checks as the most expensive, with a $2,583 annual price tag"

— Empower (June 2025). https://www.empower.com/the-currency/play/game-on-the-40-billion-play-in-youth-sports-news 
The "this is straining families" / equity angle
The cost is pushing families into debt

"A Good Sports–Harris Poll report found 75% of parents considered pulling kids from sports, 58% said equipment is a financial strain, and nearly one in five took on debt."

— Monee (Oct 2025). https://monee-app.com/blog/how-to-budget-for-youth-sports-with-a-seasonbyseason-plan 


Travel-sports parents are raiding their savings

"A 2025 New York Life survey found that 25% of travel sports parents have pulled from their savings or emergency fund to cover costs."

— Baseball Mode (Oct 2024). https://baseballmode.com/blogs/blog/travel-baseball-101 


The income gap is widening into a two-tier system

"The income-based participation gap has reached 20.2 percentage points: children from the wealthiest families are nearly twice as likely to play organized sports as those from the lowest-income families."

— Strive (citing Project Play 2025). https://joinstriveon.com/blog/youth-sports-business 



The cleanest "commercialization" / villain framing for the article

"Critics use the term 'youth sports industrial complex' to describe how commercialization has shifted the purpose of youth athletics from development and fun toward revenue generation."

— Strive (April 2026). https://joinstriveon.com/blog/youth-sports-business
